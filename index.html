Introduction

The Weill Cornell Medicine Center on Aging and Behavioral Research has accumulated over twenty years of data on psychosocial outcomes of caregivers and older adults with cognitive impairments, such as Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) and Alzheimerâ€™s Disease or Related Dementias (ADRD). These datasets, derived from various studies involving technology-based interventions, offer a rich resource for understanding the complex dynamics of caregiving and cognitive decline. However, these datasets are fragmented across multiple studies, each with inconsistent coding practices and diverse populations, which diminishes their analytical power and efficacy for secondary analysis.

To address this need, we are creating a hierarchically structured, queryable database to integrate the fragmented datasets at Weill Cornell Medicine. A unified database will facilitate comprehensive analyses, allowing researchers to test hypotheses and detect subtle effects that are often obscured in isolated studies. This resource is particularly valuable for early career researchers at Weill Cornell Medicine, providing them with access to robust datasets that can support a wide range of research initiatives. Furthermore, a well-organized database can serve as a core resource that can be expanded to include additional datasets from external researchers, thereby enhancing collaborative efforts and broadening the scope of available data.

Our Work

Our project focuses on integrating several key datasets: Resources for Enhancing Alzheimerâ€™s Caregiver Health, 1996-2001 (REACH 1), Resources for Enhancing Alzheimerâ€™s Caregiver Health II (REACH 2), Resources for Enhancing Alzheimerâ€™s Caregiver Health in the Community (Community REACH), Caring for the Caregiver Network (NINR), Grants for Early Medical/Surgical Specialistsâ€™ Transition to Aging Research (GEMSSTAR), and the Video Network Study Assessment (VIDEO). We organized and standardized the data tables, ensuring uniformity and compatibility across different studies. Extensive data wrangling was conducted to clean and transform the data into a unified format, addressing inconsistencies and integrating disparate data sources. A robust database architecture was designed to support efficient querying and data retrieval, enabling researchers to easily access and analyze the data. To facilitate broad access, we developed a web application that provides a user-friendly interface for querying the database.
Our project has made some progress in improving the accessibility and utility of psychosocial data on caregivers and older adults with cognitive impairments. By integrating these 5 key datasets (Due to inconsistencies and small sample sizes, the GEMSSTAR database was removed to ensure uniformity and prevent skewed results), we have standardized 1,277 variables related to caregiving and cognitive impairments. Additionally, we established a web interface to facilitate easy access and querying of the database for researchers. Our work supports future research on psychosocial outcomes, caregiving, and cognitive impairments by providing a comprehensive and accessible data resource. This integrated database represents a valuable advancement in the field, significantly enhancing research capabilities at Weill Cornell Medicine and beyond. It lays the groundwork for further studies aimed at improving the lives of caregivers and older adults with cognitive impairments, fostering a deeper understanding of the psychosocial dynamics involved. By enabling meta-analytic approaches, this resource can help identify subtle effects and interactions that might be missed in smaller, isolated studies, ultimately leading to more informed and effective interventions and policies.
